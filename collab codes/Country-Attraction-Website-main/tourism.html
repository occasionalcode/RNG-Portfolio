Egypt's
Top 5 Attractions

Great Pyramid of Giza

The Great Pyramid of Giza (also known as the Pyramid of Khufu or the Pyramid of Cheops) is the oldest and largest of the Giza pyramid complex, which borders modern-day Giza in Egypt. It is the oldest of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World, as well as the only one that has been preserved in its entirety. The Great Pyramid was constructed by quarrying 2.3 million massive pieces weighing a total of 6 million tonnes. The majority of the stones aren't uniform in size or shape, and they're just lightly treated.

Abu Simbel Temples

Abu Simbel is an ancient temple complex in southern Egypt, built into a solid rock cliff and situated near the Nile River's second cataract. The two temples that make up the site were built either between 1264 and 1244 BCE or 1244-1224 BCE under the reign of Ramesses II (c. 1279 - c. 1213 BCE). The Great Temple is 98 feet (30 meters) tall and 115 feet (35 meters) long, with four sitting colossi flanking the entrance, two on each side, each 65 feet (20 meters) tall, showing Ramesses II on his throne.

Nile River

Until it empties into the Mediterranean Sea, the Nile River travels for 6,600 kilometers (4,100 miles). The river has offered a supply of irrigation for thousands of years, transforming the parched plains surrounding it into lush agricultural land. The river is still used as a source of irrigation as well as a major transit and trading route today.

Saqqara

In English, Saccara/Saqqara is an Egyptian hamlet in the Giza Governorate notable for its huge, ancient burial site of Egyptian pharaohs (kings) and royals, which serves as the necropolis for Memphis, the ancient Egyptian metropolis. There are many pyramids at Saqqara, including the world-famous Step pyramid of Djoser, also known as the Step Tomb, and a number of mastaba tombs. Saqqara is around 7 by 1.5 km in size and is located 30 kilometers (19 miles) south of modern-day Cairo (4.3 by 0.9 mi).

Siwa Oasis

Siwa Oasis is located 350 miles (560 kilometers) west-southwest of Cairo, near the Libyan border. The oasis is 6 miles (10 kilometers) long and 4–5 miles (6–8 kilometers) broad, with 200 springs. The historic walled villages of Siwa and Aghrm, which are true fortresses, are located on two rock outcrops. The oasis was the location of the oracle temple of Amon (Zeus Ammon), which was already famous in the time of Herodotus and was consulted by Alexander the Great. Siwa's ancient Egyptian name was Sekht-am, which meant "palm country."
